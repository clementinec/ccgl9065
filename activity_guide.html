Activity Guide

CCGL9065: How In-Class Activities Work

Author

Dr. Hongshan Guo

30-Second Summary
  • Level 0 (Week 1): Two-side teaser debate — jump in, get a taste
  • Level 1 (Week 2+): Small vocational groups, individual 5-min presentations
  • Level 2 (unlocked): Cross-room debates when the class votes ready
  • Participation: Present 3 times → full 10% (Week 1 + Week 12 + one other)
  • Core skill: Turn facts into spectacles using the formula: Fact + Human Story + Stakes

Semester Structure

Level When Format Goal
Level 0 Week 1 only Two-side teaser debate Get a taste of cross-room confrontation
Level 1 Week 2+ (default) Vocational groups, individual presentations Build persona, practice spectacles
Level 2 Unlocked by class Cross-room Assembly Debates Hardcore argumentation and cross-fire

How Level 2 Gets Unlocked

We don’t rush into Assembly Debates — the class earns it.

  1. Slack tracker: A pinned post in the Slack workspace tracks interest in “Level 2”
  2. Like to vote: React to the post if you want to try Level 2
  3. Threshold reached: When enough likes accumulate, we kick off Level 2 the following week
  4. After the experiment: Class votes in person — continue Level 2, or fall back to Level 1 if it’s too much

Level 0: Week 1 Teaser

Level 0 at a Glance

When: Week 1 only | Format: Full class, two sides | Vibe: Chaotic but energizing

In Week 1, we skip straight to a simplified two-side debate:

  • Left side of room: PRO-AI (Pro-Development)
  • Right side of room: PRO-US (Pro-Climate)

Note: From Week 2 onwards, we drop the AI framing and use Pro-Dev vs Pro-CC — same idea, broader scope.

This is a teaser — chaotic, unpolished, but energizing. It shows you what Assembly Debates feel like before you’ve built the skills. Counts toward your participation if you speak.

After Week 1, we step back and build properly.

Level 1: Vocational Groups

Level 1 at a Glance

When: Week 2 onwards (default) | Format: Small groups by occupation | Vibe: Skill-building, low pressure

How It Works

Students are organized into vocational (occupation-based) groups:

Group Perspective
Logistics/Transportation Supply chains, shipping, mobility
Farming/Food Procurement Agriculture, food systems, land use
Energy and Industry Power generation, manufacturing
Policy-Makers Governance, regulation, international agreements
Technology and Innovation Tech solutions, R&D, digital tools
Service/Urban Workers Cities, daily life, consumer perspective

Choosing Your Group

  • Week 2: You’ll be assigned a starting group based on seating
  • Any week after: You can stay with your group or switch to a different one
  • Switching is fine — maybe you want to explore a new perspective, or a topic fits another vocation better
  • Staying is also fine — depth in one persona can be just as valuable as breadth

Just move to the new group’s seating area at the start of the activity. No permission needed.

Within Each Group

Students choose their stance based on their collected research profile:

  • Pro-Climate (Pro-CC): Prioritize environmental protection, sustainability
  • Pro-Development (Pro-Dev): Prioritize economic growth, pragmatic progress

Weekly Activity Flow (50 min)

Phase Time What Happens
Settle into Groups 2 min Find your vocational group by seating
Position Exchange 3 min Quick round: declare Pro-CC or Pro-Dev, share one-line take
Group Presentations 35 min Each group presents (~5 min max per group)
Wrap-Up 5 min Reactions, standout moments, preview next week
Buffer 5 min Overflow / transition time
On Presentation Length

5 minutes is the maximum, not a target. Brevity is memorable — if you can land your point in 2 minutes, do it. Each of the 6 vocational groups should get a chance to present each week.

The 5-Minute Presentation

Presentation Structure (click to expand)
Segment Time Content
Hook 30 sec Open with your most powerful story or image
Argument 1 90 sec Your strongest point as a spectacle
Argument 2 90 sec Your second-strongest point
Argument 3 60 sec Third point or preemptive defense
Close 30 sec Call to action or memorable line

Presenter Tips:

  • Don’t read — know your points, speak naturally
  • Make eye contact — with the audience, not your notes
  • Use pauses — let your big moments land
  • End strong — last line should be memorable

Persona Creation

Your vocational group is the foundation of your persona — use it as a label that grounds your arguments.

Answer these questions:

  1. Who are you? (Name, job within your vocational group, location)
  2. What’s your daily reality? (How does climate/development affect your work?)
  3. What do you fear losing? (Job? Community? Future?)
  4. What do you hope for? (What’s your vision of success?)

Think of your vocational label as your argumentative superpower: “As a logistics worker…” or “Speaking from the farming sector…” gives your points immediate credibility and specificity.

Example Personas (click to expand)

Pro-CC Farmer: > “I’m Maria, a rice farmer in the Mekong Delta. Every year the floods get worse. Last year I lost 40% of my harvest. My children want to move to the city. I fear my family’s land — farmed for three generations — will be underwater by 2050.”

Pro-Dev Energy Worker: > “I’m James, a power plant technician in Guangdong. My plant employs 200 families from my village. When people talk about shutting down coal, they’re talking about my neighbors losing their homes. I want clean energy too — but not if it means my community starves first.”

Participation Tracking

How to Get Full Marks (10%)

Present 3 times throughout the semester. That’s it.

Week Opportunity
Week 1 Level 0 teaser debate — counts if you speak
Weeks 2–11 Level 1 or Level 2 presentations
Week 12 Portfolio presentation — counts for everyone

Realistically: Week 1 + Week 12 + one other week = done.

Level 2: Assembly Debates

Level 2 at a Glance

When: Unlocked by class vote | Format: Full class, cross-room | Vibe: High-stakes, deploy your skills

The Shift

After weeks of building comfort with personas and individual presentations, the final sessions bring the class together for cross-room debates.

What Changes Level 1 Level 2
Room Layout Groups by occupation Two sides: PRO-CC vs PRO-DEV
Format Individual presentations Cross-fire debates across the room
Intensity Warmup, exploration High-stakes argumentation
Goal Build skills Deploy skills

Vocational Groups Still Matter

You keep your vocational identity in Level 2 — it becomes part of your persona:

  • “As someone in Energy and Industry, I see this differently…”
  • “From a Policy-Maker’s perspective, the real issue is…”
  • “Speaking as a farmer, let me tell you what this means on the ground…”

Your vocational lens makes your arguments more grounded and specific.

Assembly Debate Flow (50 min)

Phase Time What Happens
Form Sides 2 min Move to your side of the room (PRO-CC left, PRO-DEV right)
Vocational Check-in 3 min Within your side, note which vocational groups are represented
Strategy Session 10 min Pool best arguments, leverage vocational perspectives
Presentation Prep 10 min Craft your side’s case
Team Presentations 10 min Each side presents (5 min each)
Cross-Fire 12 min Debate across the room — rebuttals, questions, challenges
Debrief 3 min What worked? What surprised you?

Team Roles (Level 2)

Role Responsibility
Facilitator Keeps time, ensures everyone contributes
Researcher Pulls key facts, checks sources
Storyteller Turns facts into narratives using the Spectacle Formula
Devil’s Advocate Stress-tests arguments: “But what about…?”
Presenter Delivers the team’s case

Open Debate Rules

Rules & Useful Phrases (click to expand)

Rules of Engagement

  1. Address the argument, not the person
  2. Ask questions, don’t just attack
  3. Concede good points — “That’s fair, but…”
  4. Redirect, don’t deny — “Even if that’s true, it doesn’t address…”

Useful Phrases

To Do Say
Challenge a claim “Where’s the source for that?”
Reframe “You’re focusing on X, but the real issue is Y”
Concede and counter “That’s a valid concern, but consider…”
Call out exaggeration “That’s one case. Is it representative?”
Redirect “Even if true, does that justify…?”

Core Skills (All Levels)

The Spectacle Formula

The Formula

\[\text{Fact} + \text{Human Story} + \text{Stakes} = \text{Spectacle}\]

Levels of Argument

Level Example Impact
Weak “AI uses energy” Forgettable
Better “AI uses more electricity than Pakistan” Surprising
Spectacle “Your ChatGPT query costs a town its drinking water” Unforgettable

How to Build a Spectacle

  1. Start with a fact — verified, sourced, real
  2. Find the human — who is affected? Name them if possible
  3. Raise the stakes — what’s at risk? What’s lost?
  4. Make it personal — connect to your audience’s life

The Line: Persuasion vs. Propaganda

Know the Difference
Persuasion (OK) Propaganda (NOT OK)
Selecting real facts Inventing facts
Framing verified stories Fabricating stories
Making real data vivid Exaggerating beyond evidence
Sourced statistics Made-up numbers

Before you use any story, ask:

  1. Can I cite a source?
  2. Could someone fact-check this and find it’s true?
  3. Am I framing reality or inventing it?

If you can’t answer YES to all three — don’t use it.

The Goal

You’re not here to “win” debates. You’re here to:

  1. Build comfort — with role-play, public speaking, argumentation
  2. Understand all sides — argue PRO-CC one week, PRO-DEV the next
  3. Develop narrative skills — turn data into stories people feel
  4. Recognize persuasion — know it when you see it, know how to do it
  5. Stay honest — powerful arguments that are also true
The Bottom Line

Facts don’t change minds. Stories do. Your job: Tell true stories that move people.

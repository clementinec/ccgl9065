Activity Guide

CCGL9065: How to Run Weekly Debates

Author

Dr. Hongshan Guo

This guide explains the structure of weekly in-class activities. Keep this handy throughout the semester.

The Weekly Rhythm

Every week follows the same pattern:

Phase Time What Happens
Lecture 50 min Facts, stakes, ammunition for both sides
Activity 50 min Groups form, synthesize, present, debate

Activity Flow (50 min)

The Standard Structure

Phase Time What to Do
Form Groups 2 min Split into PRO and CON sides
Assign Roles 3 min Each person takes a role
Persona Creation 5 min Who are you? What’s your story?
Research Synthesis 10 min Pool your homework, build arguments
Presentation Prep 10 min Craft your 3 key points as stories
Presentations 10 min Each side presents (5 min each)
Open Debate 8 min Cross-fire, rebuttals, questions
Debrief 2 min Key takeaways, what worked

Group Roles

Assign these at the start of each activity. Rotate weekly so everyone tries different roles.

Role Responsibility
Facilitator Keeps time, moves discussion forward, ensures everyone contributes
Researcher Pulls key facts from everyone’s homework, checks sources
Storyteller Turns facts into narratives using the Spectacle Formula
Devil’s Advocate Stress-tests arguments: “But what about…?”
Presenter Delivers the group’s case to the class

Why Roles Matter

Without roles, groups default to: - One person doing all the work - Circular discussions that go nowhere - Arguments that stay at the “fact” level

With roles: - Everyone has a job - Discussion has direction - Arguments become stories

The Spectacle Formula

This is how you turn facts into arguments that land.

\[\text{Fact} + \text{Human Story} + \text{Stakes} = \text{Spectacle}\]

Levels of Argument

Level Example Impact
Weak “AI uses energy” Forgettable
Better “AI uses more electricity than Pakistan” Surprising
Spectacle “Your ChatGPT query costs a town its drinking water” Unforgettable

How to Build a Spectacle

  1. Start with a fact — verified, sourced, real
  2. Find the human — who is affected? Name them if possible
  3. Raise the stakes — what’s at risk? What’s lost?
  4. Make it personal — connect to your audience’s life

The Line: Persuasion vs. Propaganda

Persuasion (OK) Propaganda (NOT OK)
Selecting real facts Inventing facts
Framing verified stories Fabricating stories
Making real data vivid Exaggerating beyond evidence
Sourced statistics Made-up numbers

Before you use any story, ask:

  1. Can I cite a source?
  2. Could someone fact-check this and find it’s true?
  3. Am I framing reality or inventing it?

If you can’t answer YES to all three — don’t use it.

Research Synthesis (10 min)

This is where your pre-class homework becomes group ammunition.

The Flow

Step Time What to Do
Share 3 min Everyone reads out their best finding from Slack/Notion
Sort 3 min Group findings by theme: Energy? Jobs? Equity?
Select 2 min Pick your 3 strongest facts
Story 2 min Storyteller drafts narratives for each

Synthesis Questions

  • What’s the most surprising fact we have?
  • What’s the most human story we can tell?
  • What fact do we have that the other side can’t counter?
  • What’s our weakest point and how do we defend it?

Presentation Prep (10 min)

Structure Your 5 Minutes

Segment Time Content
Hook 30 sec Open with your most powerful story/image
Argument 1 90 sec Your strongest point as a spectacle
Argument 2 90 sec Your second-strongest point
Argument 3 60 sec Third point or preemptive defense
Close 30 sec Call to action or memorable line

Presenter Tips

  • Don’t read — know your points, speak naturally
  • Make eye contact — with the other side, not just your team
  • Use pauses — let your big moments land
  • End strong — last line should be memorable

Open Debate (8 min)

Rules of Engagement

  1. Address the argument, not the person
  2. Ask questions, don’t just attack
  3. Concede good points — “That’s fair, but…”
  4. Redirect, don’t deny — “Even if that’s true, it doesn’t address…”

Useful Phrases

To Do Say
Challenge a claim “Where’s the source for that?”
Reframe “You’re focusing on X, but the real issue is Y”
Concede and counter “That’s a valid concern, but consider…”
Call out exaggeration “That’s one case. Is it representative?”
Redirect “Even if true, does that justify…?”

Debrief (2 min)

Quick reflection at the end:

  • What argument from the other side actually worked on you?
  • What was your side’s weakest moment?
  • What would you do differently next time?

  3. Patterns emerge — you’ll see what kinds of arguments you’re good at

The Goal

You’re not here to “win” debates. You’re here to:

  1. Understand all sides — argue PRO one week, CON the next
  2. Build narrative skills — turn data into stories people feel
  3. Recognize persuasion — know it when you see it, know how to do it
  4. Stay honest — powerful arguments that are also true

Facts don’t change minds. Stories do. Your job: Tell true stories that move people.
